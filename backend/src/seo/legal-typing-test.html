The Secret Cost of Slow Typing in Law

In the legal profession, words are the fundamental currency. Whether you are drafting a motion, responding to discovery, or entering billing narratives, the speed at which your brain can transfer thought to the document directly impacts your firm's profitability and your own daily stress levels.

For paralegals and legal assistants, achieving a verifiable speed of 65-80 WPM is often the baseline requirement for employment. For attorneys, slow typing extends the workday by hours; a lawyer typing at 40 WPM spends twice as much time drafting an appellate brief as one typing at 80 WPM.

The Typing Tax: If you spend 4 hours a day typing at 40 WPM, increasing your speed to 80 WPM buys you back exactly two full hours every single day.

Why Standard Typing Tests Are Inadequate

Practicing your typing speed using standard tests completely entirely ignores the realities of legal documentation.

Legal professionals do not type simple narratives. The legal vocabulary is dense with Latin phrases (res ipsa loquitur, subpoena duces tecum), highly specific terms of art (indemnification, estoppel), and an enormous volume of proper nouns and highly specific capitalization rules.

If your fingers aren't specifically trained to rapidly execute legal bigrams and trigrams, your typing rhythm breaks every time you encounter terminology outside of conversational English. This constant stuttering destroys your overall WPM and dramatically increases your error rate.

Mastering Legal Citations & Symbols

Perhaps the biggest hurdle in legal typing is the formatting and citation structure. The Bluebook rules require an absolute mastery of the symbol keys and the top number row. Consider typing this string rapidly:

Smith v. Jones, 123 F.3d 456, 458-59 (9th Cir. 2004) (§ 1983 claim)

This single citation requires mastery of the shift key, rapid toggling of the section symbol (§), precise number row strikes, and complex parenthetical groupings. A generic typing test will never properly assess or train your ability to execute these maneuvers cleanly. Only a dedicated legal typing test can build the necessary accuracy required for true speed.
